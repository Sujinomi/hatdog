Task 1: Design Principles 1.1 KISS (Keep It Simple, Stupid) 1.2 KISS (Keep It Simple, Stupid) - is a design principle which states that designs and/or systems should be as simple as possible. Wherever possible, complexity should be avoided in a system -- as simplicity guarantees the greatest levels of user acceptance and interaction. KISS is used in a variety of disciplines, such as interface design, product design, and software development. The term was first used in the US Navy and is thought to have been coined by Kelly Johnson, who was the lead engineer at the Lockheed Skunk Works. Johnson told the designers at Lockheed that their designs should be simple enough to be repaired by a man in a combat situation with only some basic mechanic’s training and simple tools. If their products weren’t simple and easy to understand, they would not only cost lives but also quickly become obsolete in combat conditions and thus worthless. In the world of user experience design and related disciplines, the KISS principle borrows from such a scenario in that users who tend to lead busy lives will quickly abandon a complex design. In the case of designing for mobile devices—where the users’ context finds them operating their phones with their fingers, often with one hand—this philosophy is even more vital to follow. 1.3 The KISS (Keep It Simple, Stupid) method in the military refers to the principle of designing systems and strategies that are simple, straightforward, and devoid of unnecessary complexity. The method emphasizes the importance of easily understandable and executable plans and designs to avoid misunderstandings, errors, and complications, ensuring the swift and effective accomplishment of objectives. This principle is universally applicable and is valued in numerous fields, including design, for its emphasis on clarity and ease of use. Instead of creating objects directly, we use a factory method to create and return them. This Design Pattern promotes code reuse and loose coupling between classes making our code more modular and easier to maintain. It enables us to create objects without knowing the implementation details of our classes. The Factory Method pattern defines an interface for creating objects, but it allows subclasses to decide which class to instantiate. It encapsulates the object creation logic in a separate method, which can be overridden by subclasses to create different types of objects. This pattern is useful when we want to create objects that belong to a single family of classes and defer instantiation to their subclasses. The Factory Method Design Pattern is a creational pattern that provides an interface for creating objects but allows subclasses to decide which class to instantiate. It is a pattern that promotes loose coupling between classes and promotes code reuse. The Factory Method Design Pattern is particularly useful when we don’t know the exact class of object that will be created at runtime. Many websites fall short in this aspect, either by employing overly complex menu structures or failing to provide clear, intuitive paths for users to find the content they seek. A confusing navigation system can frustrate visitors and result in higher bounce rates. - Lack of Mobile Responsiveness: Failing to optimize for mobile devices can result in a poor user experience. Statista’s findings that 61% of organic searches come from smartphones. Other research further asserts that this trend is still on the rise. What does that mean for you? Among other things, it means that there is a big chance your target customers are searching for your content via mobile devices. If you bring your website's responsiveness to the next level, future leads will be able to find you easier. As the use of mobile device continues to grow, it's crucial for websites to be responsive, adapting seamlessly to different screen sizes. Sites that aren't optimized for mobile can lead to a subpar user experience, with elements not displaying correctly or requiring excessive zooming and scrolling. - Slow Loading Times: Slow-loading websites can drive users away and negatively impact SEO. In today's fast-paced digital world, users expect websites to load quickly. Slow-loading pages can lead to user impatience and abandonment, adversely affecting a site's bounce rate and search engines ranking. Image and code optimization is vital to improving load times. - Inconsistent Design: Incoherent design elements across a site can be disorienting. A cohesive design is essential for creating a professional and visually appealing websites. Websites that lack consistency in fonts, colors, and layouts can appear unpolished and confused users. Consistency is also crucial for building a strong brand identity. - Overwhelming Content: Overcrowded pages with too much content can overwhelm visitors. An overload of content, crowded layouts, or excessive pop-ups can overwhelm and discourage users. Cluttered websites make it difficult for visitors to focus on the information they need and can negatively impact the user experience. - Ignoring Accessibility: Failing to provide accessible features can exclude users with disabilities. Web accessibility is not only a legal requirement in many regions but also a matter of inclusion. Websites that don't take accessibility into account can exclude individuals with disabilities, such as those with visual of hearing impairments. Simple design adjustments and alternative text for images can go a long way in improving accessibility. - Poor Typography: Illegible fonts and poor contrast affect readability. - Lack of Contact Information: Transparency and trust are vital in online interactions. When users interact with a website it's crucial to provide a feedback or error messages.
